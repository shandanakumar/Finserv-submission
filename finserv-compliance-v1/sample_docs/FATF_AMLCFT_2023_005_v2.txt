RECOMMENDATION 10 — CUSTOMER DUE DILIGENCE
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Financial institutions should be prohibited from keeping anonymous accounts or accounts
in obviously fictitious names.

Financial institutions should be required to undertake Customer Due Diligence (CDD)
measures when:
  (a) Establishing business relations;
  (b) Carrying out occasional transactions above USD/EUR 15,000 (whether a single
      transaction or linked transactions);
  (c) There is a suspicion of money laundering or terrorist financing;
  (d) The financial institution has doubts about the veracity or adequacy of previously
      obtained customer identification data.

CDD Measures Required:

(i) Identifying the customer and verifying that customer's identity using reliable,
    independent source documents, data or information;

(ii) Identifying the beneficial owner and taking reasonable measures to verify the
     identity of the beneficial owner, such that the financial institution is satisfied
     that it knows who the beneficial owner is; for legal persons and arrangements,
     understanding the ownership and control structure of the customer;

(iii) Understanding and, as appropriate, obtaining information on the purpose and
      intended nature of the business relationship;

(iv) Conducting ongoing due diligence on the business relationship and scrutiny of
     transactions undertaken throughout the course of that relationship to ensure that
     the transactions being conducted are consistent with the institution's knowledge
     of the customer, their business and risk profile, including, where necessary,
     the source of funds.

Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD):

Financial institutions should be required to apply EDD measures, on a risk-sensitive
basis, in situations that by their nature can present a higher risk of money laundering
or terrorist financing, including:
  — High-risk third countries as identified by FATF (currently including Iran, North Korea,
    and countries on the FATF grey list);
  — Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs);
  — Correspondent banking relationships.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
RECOMMENDATION 12 — POLITICALLY EXPOSED PERSONS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

In addition to performing normal CDD measures, financial institutions should be required to:

(a) Have appropriate risk management systems to determine whether the customer or the
    beneficial owner is a politically exposed person;

(b) For foreign PEPs — obtain senior management approval for establishing or continuing
    business relationships with such persons;

(c) Take reasonable measures to establish the source of wealth and source of funds of
    customers and beneficial owners identified as PEPs;

(d) Conduct enhanced, ongoing monitoring of the business relationship.

For domestic PEPs and persons entrusted with a prominent function by an international
organisation, financial institutions should apply the measures above where higher risk
is identified.

RECOMMENDATION 16 — WIRE TRANSFERS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Countries should ensure that financial institutions include required and accurate
originator information and required beneficiary information, on wire transfers and
related messages, and that the information remains with the wire transfer or related
message throughout the payment chain.

Required Originator Information:
  — Name;
  — Account number (or unique transaction reference);
  — Address, national identity number, customer identification number, or date and
    place of birth.

Required Beneficiary Information:
  — Name;
  — Account number (or unique transaction reference).

High-Risk Wire Transfers:
For wire transfers of USD/EUR 1,000 or more where the ordering institution cannot
comply with the above requirements, the beneficiary institution must consider restricting
or rejecting the transaction and filing a Suspicious Transaction Report (STR).

Cross-Border Transfers:
Financial institutions should verify that required originator and beneficiary information
is complete for all cross-border wire transfers of USD/EUR 1,000 and above.
Incomplete transfers must be subject to enhanced scrutiny and potentially rejected.

RECOMMENDATION 20 — REPORTING OF SUSPICIOUS TRANSACTIONS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

If a financial institution suspects or has reasonable grounds to suspect that funds are
the proceeds of a criminal activity, or are related to terrorist financing, it should
be required, by law, to report promptly its suspicions to the Financial Intelligence
Unit (FIU).

Key obligations:
  (a) Report must be filed regardless of whether the firm proceeds with or refuses
      the transaction;
  (b) Financial institutions and their directors, officers, and employees must be
      protected by law from criminal and civil liability for breach of confidentiality
      when reporting in good faith;
  (c) Tipping-off prohibition: firms must not disclose to the customer or third parties
      that a STR has been or will be filed.

HIGH-RISK JURISDICTIONS — FATF GREY LIST (as at February 2023)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Countries subject to FATF's increased monitoring (grey list) require application of
Enhanced Due Diligence by all FATF member jurisdictions:

Albania, Barbados, Burkina Faso, Cameroon, Cayman Islands, Democratic Republic of Congo,
Croatia, Haiti, Jamaica, Jordan, Mali, Mozambique, Myanmar, Nigeria, Panama,
Philippines, Senegal, South Africa, South Sudan, Syria (also on black list),
Tanzania, Turkey, Uganda, United Arab Emirates, Yemen.

Countries subject to a call for action (black list — highest risk):
Iran, Democratic People's Republic of Korea (DPRK / North Korea).

Note: Grey list is updated at each FATF plenary (typically February, June, October).
Financial institutions must consult the FATF website for the most current list.
